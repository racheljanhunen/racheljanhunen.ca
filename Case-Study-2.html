Dashlight
A Restaurant Menu Platform

This product was designed to improve the experience of customers at a sit-down restaurant. The solution includes an interface designed for a tablet, that resides at each table. The goals are to present all of the menu items in a manner that aids in decision making and improve COVID-19 safety between customers and servers. The development of this project took place with a design team at the University of Waterloo.

My Role

The roles I had in this design were persona development, user requirements and functional requirements development, low and medium fidelity wireframes, design refinement, high fidelity wireframes, and user performance testing.

The Challenge

Through discussions with peers and reflection on individual experiences within the design group, a few frustrations with the experience at restaurants were brought to light. First, the desire to minimize contact between the server and the customer was shared, to decrease the risk of contracting COVID-19. As well, concerns for the decision making process were outlined, as it can be a lengthy process to narrow down food options when ordering. Lastly, the ordering process was also noted to be cumbersome, with a large amount of time spent waiting on the server to return to the table.

Thus, a need exists to design a platform that targets these problem areas, to create a better experience for customers at a sit-down restaurant.

User Surveys

To better understand the problem space, a survey was created, to get insight from potential users. The survey included a series of scale, short answer, and open ended questions. 

From the surveys, it was found that users would prefer to use a restaurant-provided menu over their phone, users would like to be in control of the frequency of server checkins, and users struggle to decide what to order at a restaurant.

Persona Development

The insight from the user surveys brought forth 2 personas. The first persona, Robert, is a male in the older age demographic. The focus of this persona was on the frustrations with navigating online menus from a phone, and little experience with technology. 

The second persona, Alyssa, is a female in the younger age demographic. The focus of this persona was on the desire to be informed on ingredients within the food to satisfy her dietary restrictions, and the need for a quick experience at a restuarant.

Scope

Based on the information from the user surveys and personas, usability requirements were created. These include:

Intuitiveness
Task completion rates should be at least 95% during the first time of using the application.

Efficiency
Users should be able to locate and complete a task within 10 seconds.

Usability
The interface must have a system usability scale (SUS) score of at least 70.


As well, functional requirements were created based on user feedback, which outlines what the prototype must include. These requirements are:

  • ○
    Interface must display a running tab
  • ○
    Interface must allow for surver-customer interaction requests
  • ○
    Interface must provide a shortlist to aid in decision making
  • ○
    Interface must display ingredients and dietary restrictions for food items
  • ○
    Interface must allow for the bill to be requested

Navigation Design

In order to design the information architecture of the platform, the series of tasks must be understood. To do this, a task analysis was created on 2 expected tasks. The first task was, "Order food at a restaurant as someone who is allergic to peanuts", and the second task was, "Pay for the meal using a credit card". Creating these task analyses made sure that no intermediary steps would be missed when creating wireframes.

Wireframing

A low fidelity wireframe was mocked up, to get a sense of the overall layout. Six screens were created, being the main menu, the menu categories, the menu filter, the search, the running tab, and the menu shortlist.

From here, a medium fidelity wireframe was created in Figma. The focus of this wireframe was on location of pieces of information, format of design features, and emphasis on call-to-action areas.

Design Refinement

Moodboard
To get a look and feel for the interface, a moodboard was created. A specific restaurant was focused on for this, being the Keg. The Keg's atmosphere and branding has a dark, rich, and romantic feel. As well, she shapes are round and organic. This is reflected in the designed moodboard.

Colour and Typography
From the moodboard, a monochromatic colour scheme was developed. The colour scheme was centred around the colour of the Keg's logo, "Raw Sienna". Salience was emphasized with the incorporation of lighter and brighter hues.

As well, sans-serif fonts were chosen to communicate a bold and professional interface. "Rubik" was chosen for page titles, to provide contrast. "Helvetica" bold and regular were chosen for the page body, to improve readibility.

Icons
The creation of icons included representational, abstract, and semi abstract. The design of the icons was flat, to improve readibility and to prevent the icons as acting as a distraction.

Designed Solution

Incorporation of the mockups and the visual design elements, a prototype was developed. The first screen outlines the main menu, with the ability to scroll through each of the menu sections. Each food item displays a photo, description, dietary icons, the option to shortlist the item, and the option to view more information about the item's ingredients. The second screen shows the menu categories pop-up when the hamburger menu icon on the left is clicked. This feature allows the user to jump to categories, rather than scrolling through the entire main menu.

The third screen  displays the filter pop-up, which appears when the filter icon on the right is clicked. This allows for the user to filter the menu based on their dietary preferences. The fourth screen is the search functionality, which appears when the magnifying glass icon is clicked. This page gives suggested searches, and auto-fills in options as the user types, improving decision making through user recognition over recall.

The fifth screen displays the shortlist page, which shows items that the user has decided to shortlist. The user is also able to remove items if they no longer wish to consider them in their decision making process. Finally, the sixth screen shows the running tab for the table. This allows the user to remain informed throughout the entire dining experience. As well, the user is able to request that the server bring the bill once the table is ready, to decrease the waiting times.

Usability Testing

To analyze the usability requirements, performance testing was implemented. A sample size of 5 with varying demographics was recruited. A series of 5 tasks was completed by each of the users on the final prototype. These tasks included instructions such as, "Add the Vegetarian Meatloaf to your shortlist", and "You have finished your dining experience, request that the server brings the bill over to the table". The task time and task completion were recorded for each task, and each user completed the SUS after all tasks were completed.

The results from the testing were averaged, and it was found that:

  • ○
    The task completion rate was 100%
  • ○
    The task completion time was 6.53s
  • ○
    The SUS score was 84.5

Outcome

From the performance testing, it can be seen that all of the usability goals were met. The task completion rate exceeded the benchmark of 95%, the task completion time fell below the benchmark of 10s, and the SUS score exceeded the benchmark of 70. As well, all of the functional requirements were incorporated into the design of the final prototype.

With the creation of "Dashlight", the customer experience at sit-down restaurants can be signficantly improved.
