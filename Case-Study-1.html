SleepTracker Case Study

Overview

This case study outlines the design approach to an interface design that accompanied a device. This product was designed to increase the ability for a caretaker to appropriately care for a person with dementia who suffers from disrupted sleep. The development of this project took place with a design team at the University of Waterloo. 

Problem

Within the community of those who have dementia, a large portion suffer from disrupted sleep. The disruption to sleep leads to an irregular sleep schedule, causing agitation, restlessness, and increased sleeping during the day. At night, this can lead to sleep apnea, calling out to caretakers, and wandering around. The disturbance to sleep negatively affects the caretaker of someone with dementia, as they are required to react to their changing needs and monitor safety through the night. Thus, a need exists for a solution that allows for the caretaker to better anticipate how the person that they are caring for will behave based on the sleep they obtained.

Users

The primary user of the designed device is the caretaker of a person with dementia. Caretakers can be family members, personal support workers, and nurses. The caretaker will be the user that is most often interacting with the developed product, and are who the product is centred around. The secondary user would be the person with dementia, as they interact with the device part of the time, specifically when it comes to monitoring sleep. 

My Responsibilities

The roles I had in this design were largely in user research and usability testing. I was responsible for conducting user research to develop personas, and information architecture testing on the interface prototype. 

Scope

To further define the scope of the project, the intended user for the final solution is primarily the caretaker, with occasional interactions with the person with dementia. The solution is also intended to be used within the place of residence of the person with dementia. Usability goals were determined, in order to assess the final product, which were:

Effective to use
The user must be able to detect nighttime wandering with minimal disruption to their sleep. To record this, the false alarm rate of the device shall be measured.

Efficient to use
The user must find that navigation of the device is instinctive. To record this, task completion rate of various navigation-related tasks will be measured.

Easy to learn
The user must be able to find the device to be intuitive, and not require much additional documentation to use it. To record this, the length it takes to complete various tasks (task time) will be measured.

As well, user experience goals were developed, to centre the design process around the feelings of the user. The goals were:

Convenient: Users should feel like using the device is convenient to their daily lifestyle
Reliable: Users should feel like the device is reliable in alerting them when the person with dementia is wandering throughout the night
Helpful: Users should feel as though the device is helping them in their caretaking abilities

Design Process

The first step in addressing this problem space was to understand the users. I developed proto-personas for both the caretaker and the person with dementia, to allow for the team to have an introductory understanding of who we were designing for. Next, I utilized secondary research, interviews with users, and the modified fly-on-the-wall technique to develop research based personas. The persona for both the caretaker and the person with dementia can be seen below.

Next, Figma was utilized to develop a prototype of the user interface. Popular interface designs were assessed, and interface design principles were implemented to achieve the design. The prototyped interface can be seen below.

Following the development of the prototyped user interface, I conducted testing on the information architecture through the form of Tree Testing. A Treejack was created, with 3 different tasks that required users to navigate through the information architecture. Seven users were recruited to take part in testing, and the results for each of the tasks can be seen below.

Outcomes

The results from the tree testing were that task 1 is the most successful in terms of the ability for users to find information. Task 2 was not successful in this area, and task 3 was the least successful. The data suggests that care should be taken in the redesign of the information architecture of tasks 2 and 3 specifically. Due to time limitations on the project's deliverable, redesign could not take place. Tree testing does an excellent job of testing the usability goals, specifically the efficient to use and easy to use goals. Based on task completion rate, task 1 meets the efficient to use goal, whereas task 2 and 3 do not. In terms of task time, the quickest time to complete was task 2 with task 3, and 1 following behind. This evaluates the easy to learn goal, with task 2 being the easiest to learn, and task 1 being the least. This design project taught the importance of user centred design, and the necessity of user testing. If this project were continued, more user testing would be conducted, specifically on the user interface itself, to gain a deeper understanding of the usability. Then, appropriate care could be taken to developing another prototype of the interface design.
